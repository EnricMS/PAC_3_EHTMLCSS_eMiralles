More about Iceland's geology

Tectonic Activity & Atlantic Ridge

Iceland's tectonic activity is largely driven by the movement of the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates, which meet and diverge along a prominent underwater mountain range called the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. This ridge runs down the center of the Atlantic Ocean, from north to south, and marks the boundary between the two plates.

As the plates move apart at the ridge, magma rises from deep within the Earth to fill the gap and create new oceanic crust. Iceland is unique in that it sits directly atop this ridge, and so experiences some of the most active and dramatic tectonic activity in the world.

This activity is responsible for the island's many volcanoes, geysers, hot springs, and other geothermal features. It also means that Iceland is constantly expanding as new crust is formed at the ridge, at a rate of about 2.5 centimeters per year.

Overall, Iceland's tectonic activity is closely tied to the ongoing processes of plate tectonics and seafloor spreading, and provides a fascinating window into the inner workings of our planet.

The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is a divergent boundary, meaning that the plates are pulling away from each other and creating new oceanic crust in the process. As magma rises to fill the gap, it solidifies and forms new seafloor. Over time, this process has led to the formation of a chain of volcanic islands and seamounts, including Iceland.

Iceland's location on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge means that it is one of the most tectonically active places on Earth, with frequent volcanic eruptions, earthquakes, and other geologic events. The island's landscape is shaped by this activity, with towering volcanoes, vast glaciers, and hot springs that bubble up from deep within the Earth's crust.

Overall, the Mid-Atlantic Ridge is an important feature of Iceland's geology, and provides a unique opportunity to study the processes of plate tectonics and seafloor spreading up close.

Volcanoes

Eyjafjallajökull: This volcano gained international attention in 2010 when it erupted and caused widespread disruption to air travel across Europe. It is located in southern Iceland and is part of a larger ice cap.

Hekla: This is one of Iceland's most active and dangerous volcanoes, located in the southern part of the country. It has erupted numerous times throughout history, with its last eruption occurring in 2000.

Katla: This is one of the largest and most active volcanoes in Iceland, located in the southern part of the country. It has erupted many times throughout history, with its last eruption occurring in 1918.

Askja: This is a large volcano located in central Iceland. It has erupted several times throughout history, with its most famous eruption occurring in 1875 when it caused widespread ashfall and famine.

Geysers

Iceland's geysers are one of the country's most iconic natural wonders. These geysers are formed by underground chambers of hot water and steam that periodically erupt in spectacular fashion, shooting boiling water and steam high into the air. The most famous of these geysers is Geysir, which gave its name to all other geysers around the world.

Located in the Haukadalur valley, Geysir is one of the oldest known geysers and has been erupting for over a thousand years. While its eruptions have become less frequent in recent decades, the nearby Strokkur geyser erupts every few minutes, shooting water up to 30 meters in the air.

Visitors to Iceland can witness the awe-inspiring power of these geysers up close, as well as enjoy the otherworldly landscapes that surround them. The geysers are a testament to the unique geological activity that occurs on this island nation, and they continue to captivate visitors from around the world.

Glaciers

Iceland is known for its stunning glaciers, which cover approximately 11% of the country's surface. Here's a plan to visit Iceland's most important glaciers:

Vatnajökull Glacier: Start your tour by visiting Vatnajökull Glacier, which is the largest glacier in Iceland and one of the largest in Europe. You can take a guided tour to explore the glacier's ice caves, crevasses, and stunning landscapes.

Langjökull Glacier: Next, head to Langjökull Glacier, which is the second largest glacier in Iceland. You can take a snowmobile tour or a guided hike on the glacier to experience its unique beauty and natural wonders.

Sólheimajökull Glacier: Visit Sólheimajökull Glacier, which is a popular destination for glacier hiking and ice climbing. You can take a guided tour to explore the glacier's ice formations and crevasses, and learn about its formation and history.

Mýrdalsjökull Glacier: Continue your tour by visiting Mýrdalsjökull Glacier, which is located in the south of Iceland. This glacier is home to Katla, one of Iceland's most active volcanoes, and is a popular destination for glacier hikes and snowmobile tours.

Snæfellsjökull Glacier: End your tour by visiting Snæfellsjökull Glacier, which is located on the Snæfellsnes Peninsula in western Iceland. This glacier is famous for its appearance in Jules Verne's novel "Journey to the Center of the Earth". You can take a guided tour to explore the glacier's stunning landscapes and unique geology.

By following this plan, you'll be able to experience Iceland's most important glaciers and explore their unique natural beauty and geological wonders. Make sure to dress warmly and wear appropriate gear for glacier hiking and snowmobiling tours.
