See the Northern Lights

Find the right time of year: The best time to see the Northern Lights in Iceland is from September to mid-April, during the winter months when the nights are longer and darker. However, it's important to note that the Northern Lights are a natural phenomenon, and there's no guarantee that you'll see them on any given night.

Find a good location: To see the Northern Lights, you'll need to be in a location with little light pollution and clear skies. Reykjavik, the capital of Iceland, is not the best place to see the Northern Lights due to light pollution, so it's best to venture out into the countryside. Some popular locations for Northern Lights viewing include Thingvellir National Park, the Snaefellsnes Peninsula, and the Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon.

Check the weather forecast: It's important to check the weather forecast before heading out to see the Northern Lights. Clear skies are essential for viewing the lights, so if the forecast calls for clouds, you may want to postpone your trip.

Book a tour: There are many tour companies in Iceland that offer Northern Lights tours, which include transportation to a good viewing location, a guide to help you spot the lights, and hot drinks to keep you warm. Booking a tour can also increase your chances of seeing the Northern Lights, as the guides are experienced in finding the best viewing spots.

Be patient: Seeing the Northern Lights is not guaranteed, and it can take some time to spot them. Be patient and give yourself plenty of time to wait for the lights to appear. It's also a good idea to bring warm clothing, as you'll be spending time outside in the cold.

Capture the moment: If you're lucky enough to see the Northern Lights, be sure to capture the moment with your camera. Use a tripod and a long exposure to capture the lights, and experiment with different settings to get the best shot.

Overall, seeing the Northern Lights in Iceland is a once-in-a-lifetime experience, and with a little planning and patience, you can increase your chances of witnessing this incredible natural phenomenon.

Relax in a hot spring

If you're looking to relax in a hot spring in Iceland, here's a plan you can follow:

Choose a hot spring: Iceland is home to many hot springs, ranging from natural pools to man-made spas. The Blue Lagoon is the most famous hot spring in Iceland, but there are also many other options to choose from, such as the Myvatn Nature Baths, the Secret Lagoon, and the Reykjadalur Hot Springs.

Book in advance: Many of the hot springs in Iceland require advance booking, especially during peak tourist season. Be sure to check the availability and book your spot in advance to avoid disappointment.

Bring the essentials: When visiting a hot spring in Iceland, it's important to bring a few essentials, such as a towel, bathing suit, and flip-flops. You may also want to bring a waterproof phone case and a waterproof camera to capture the experience.

Arrive early or late: If you want to avoid the crowds, it's best to arrive early in the morning or later in the evening. This will also give you more time to relax and enjoy the hot spring.

Follow the rules: When visiting a hot spring in Iceland, it's important to follow the rules and guidelines, such as showering before entering the pool, not bringing any food or drinks into the pool, and respecting the natural environment.

Relax and enjoy: Once you're in the hot spring, take the time to relax and enjoy the experience. Soak in the warm water, take in the stunning surroundings, and let your worries melt away.

Overall, visiting a hot spring in Iceland is a must-do activity for anyone looking to relax and unwind. By following these simple steps, you can ensure that you have a memorable and enjoyable experience.

Go whale watching

Watching whales in Iceland is a unique and unforgettable experience. Here's a plan to make the most out of your whale watching trip in Iceland:

Choose the right time: The best time to go whale watching in Iceland is from May to September. During this time, the waters are calmer and the whales are more active.

Book a tour: There are many companies that offer whale watching tours in Iceland, so it's important to do some research to find a reputable one that suits your budget and preferences. You can book your tour in advance online or in person.

Dress appropriately: The weather in Iceland can be unpredictable, so it's important to dress in layers and wear warm clothing. You may also want to bring waterproof clothing in case it rains or if you get splashed on the boat.

Bring binoculars and a camera: Binoculars will help you get a better view of the whales from a distance, and a camera will allow you to capture the moment and take some amazing photos.

Follow the rules: It's important to follow the rules and regulations of the tour operator when watching whales in Iceland. This includes not feeding or touching the whales, keeping a safe distance from them, and not disturbing their natural habitat.

Enjoy the experience: Watching whales in Iceland is an unforgettable experience, so take the time to appreciate and enjoy the moment. Keep your eyes peeled for other marine wildlife, such as dolphins and seals, and take in the beautiful scenery of Iceland's coast.

By following these tips, you'll be well prepared to enjoy a memorable whale watching experience in Iceland.

Iceland's cultural attractions

Iceland has a rich cultural heritage that can be explored through its various attractions and museums. Here's a plan to make the most out of your cultural trip to Iceland:

Reykjavik City Walk: Start your trip by taking a walking tour of Reykjavik city. You can explore some of the city's most iconic landmarks, such as Hallgrímskirkja, Harpa Concert Hall, and the Sun Voyager sculpture.

National Museum of Iceland: Next, head to the National Museum of Iceland to learn about the country's history and culture. The museum features exhibitions on Iceland's settlement, history, and modern society.

Saga Museum: Get a glimpse into Iceland's Viking past at the Saga Museum. This interactive museum features life-sized figures and exhibits depicting Iceland's early history and legends.

Icelandic Phallological Museum: For something more unusual, check out the Icelandic Phallological Museum, which houses a collection of over 280 penises and penile parts from various species, including humans.

Settlement Exhibition: Explore the ruins of a Viking Age longhouse at the Settlement Exhibition. This museum features interactive exhibits and multimedia displays that bring Iceland's early history to life.

Reykjavik Art Museum: End your cultural tour of Iceland with a visit to the Reykjavik Art Museum. The museum features a collection of Icelandic art from the 1940s to the present day, including works by famous Icelandic artists such as Erró and Georg Guðni.

By following this plan, you'll be able to explore Iceland's cultural attractions and gain a deeper understanding of the country's history and heritage.
